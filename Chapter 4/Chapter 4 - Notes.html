Part 1: Social Engineering Fundamentals

Social Engineering: The practice of manipulating people through various strategies to accomplish desired actions. Social engineers influence targets to take actions they might not otherwise have taken.

Core Principles of Social Engineering

1. Authority

Relies on people's tendency to obey someone who appears to be in charge or knowledgeable. Attackers may claim to be managers, government officials, or other authority figures.

Example: A caller claiming to be from "IT headquarters" demanding immediate password changes or system access.

2. Intimidation

Uses fear or bullying to force desired actions. Targets feel threatened and comply to avoid consequences.

Example: Threatening job loss or legal action if immediate compliance isn't given.

3. Consensus/Social Proof

Exploits people's desire to do what others are doing. May include fake testimonials or claims that "everyone else" has already complied.

Example: "All other departments have already updated their information through this link."

4. Scarcity

Creates urgency by making something appear limited or about to expire.

Example: "Only 2 spots left for this security training - register now!"

5. Familiarity

Relies on targets liking or recognizing the individual or organization being impersonated.

Example: Using company logos and email templates to appear legitimate.

6. Trust

Builds a connection with targets to encourage compliance. Unlike familiarity, this involves actively developing rapport.

Example: Engaging in small talk before making requests, referencing shared connections.

7. Urgency

Creates time pressure to prevent careful thinking and force quick decisions.

Example: "Your account will be closed in 24 hours unless you verify immediately."

Information Warfare Attacks

Misinformation: Incorrect information, often from getting facts wrong (unintentional).
Disinformation: Deliberately false information spread to serve specific goals (intentional).
Malinformation: True information used maliciously to inflict harm (MDM framework).

CISA's TRUST Process for Countering MDM

  1. Tell your story
  2. Ready your team
  3. Understand and assess MDM
  4. Strategize response
  5. Track outcomes

Infrastructure-Based Attacks

Watering Hole Attacks

Compromise websites frequently visited by targets, like animals gathering at a watering hole.

  • Attackers identify sites targets visit regularly
  • Compromise the site or its ad networks
  • Deploy malware to visitors
Example: Compromising an industry news site that employees regularly visit for updates.

Typosquatting

Register misspelled domains to catch users who make typing errors.

  • Common character substitutions (amaz0n.com)
  • Missing or extra characters (amazoon.com)
  • Wrong TLD (.co instead of .com)
üí° Defense Tip: Organizations often register common typos of their domains and redirect them to the legitimate site.

Pharming

Redirecting users to fake sites by manipulating DNS resolution.

  • Modifying hosts files
  • Compromising DNS servers
  • DNS cache poisoning

Part 3: Password Attacks

Primary Attack Methods (Exam Focus)

1. Brute Force Attacks

Definition: Iterating through passwords until finding one that works.

  • Can use word lists with common passwords
  • May include modification rules for complexity
  • Time-intensive but eventually successful if undefended
Example: Trying "password", "Password1", "Password123", etc. against a single account.

2. Password Spraying

Definition: Using a single password or small set against many accounts.

  • Avoids account lockout policies
  • Effective with default or common passwords
  • Often targets known username formats
Example: Trying "Summer2024!" against all employee accounts in an organization.
Attack Type Approach Advantages Disadvantages Detection Methods
Brute Force Many passwords ‚Üí One account Eventually finds weak passwords Triggers lockout policies, time-intensive Multiple failed logins for single account
Password Spraying One password ‚Üí Many accounts Avoids lockouts, quick results Limited password attempts Same password across multiple accounts
Dictionary Attack Word list ‚Üí Target accounts Efficient for common passwords Limited by word list quality Pattern of common passwords

Online vs. Offline Attacks

Online Attacks

  • Performed against live systems
  • Subject to rate limiting and lockouts
  • Can trigger alerts and logging
  • Limited attempts possible

Offline Attacks

  • Performed against captured password hashes
  • No rate limiting or lockouts
  • Can use rainbow tables or GPU acceleration
  • Much faster than online attacks

Password Storage and Protection

Hash: A one-way cryptographic function that generates a unique, repeatable output from an input. Cannot be reversed to obtain the original input.

Rainbow Tables

Precomputed databases of hash values for quick password lookup.

  • Only work for specific hash algorithms
  • Defeated by salting (adding random data to passwords)
  • Require significant storage space

Password Cracking Tools

John the Ripper: Popular open-source password cracking tool

  • Supports multiple hash formats
  • Includes built-in word lists
  • Can be used for password assessment
  • Supports GPU acceleration
üí° Best Practice: Use strong hashing algorithms (bcrypt, scrypt, Argon2) with salting and peppering to protect stored passwords.

Exam Tips and Strategies

Time Management

  • Social engineering questions often involve scenarios - read carefully
  • Look for keywords that indicate specific attack types
  • Password attack questions focus on patterns in logs

Common Exam Traps

  • Phishing vs. Spear Phishing: Look for targeting specificity
  • Misinformation vs. Disinformation: Intent matters (accidental vs. deliberate)
  • Password Spraying vs. Brute Force: Check the pattern (one password/many accounts vs. many passwords/one account)
  • BEC vs. Brand Impersonation: BEC uses legitimate-looking business emails, brand impersonation mimics known companies

Defense Priorities

  1. User Education: Most effective against social engineering
  2. MFA Implementation: Defeats most password attacks
  3. Email Security: Filters, authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)
  4. Verification Procedures: Callback policies, approval workflows
  5. Monitoring and Logging: Detect attack patterns early
