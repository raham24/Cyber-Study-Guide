Question 8 Answer: B - Pretexting
Why B is correct: Pretexting involves creating a fabricated scenario (the urgent event requiring gift cards) to manipulate the target into taking action. The caller created a believable pretext about a senior manager's urgent need.
Why other options are less accurate:
  • A (Phishing): This was a phone call, not email-based phishing.
  • C (BEC): While similar, BEC specifically involves business email, not phone calls.
  • D (Carding): Carding involves using stolen credit card information.
Question 9 Answer: D - The number of failed attempts for each user
Why D is correct: Password spraying typically results in a low number of failed attempts per user (often just one) across many users. This pattern distinguishes it from brute-force attacks which show many attempts against individual accounts.
Detection Pattern: Password spraying shows 1-2 failed attempts per user across many users, while brute force shows many attempts against few users.
Question 10 Answer: A - Misinformation campaigns
Why A is correct: Nation-state actors frequently use misinformation and disinformation campaigns as part of influence operations and cyber warfare. These campaigns are resource-intensive and align with nation-state objectives.
Real-World Context: Examples include election interference campaigns and social media manipulation by state-sponsored actors.
Question 11 Answer: C - Typosquatting
Why C is correct: The misspelled domain "smazon.com" (missing 'a') directing to an ad-filled site is classic typosquatting behavior. Typosquatters monetize traffic from typing errors.
Question 12 Answer: B - A watering hole attack
Why B is correct: Setting up malicious tools on the organization's primary internal website that employees frequently visit is a watering hole attack. The attacker compromises a site the targets will naturally visit.
Question 13 Answer: C - Brand impersonation
Why C is correct: Brand impersonation specifically involves pretending to be a legitimate, recognizable brand to increase trust and response rates. This goes beyond generic phishing by leveraging brand recognition.
Question 14 Answer: C - Impersonation

Why B (Pretexting) is less accurate: Pretexting was the supporting technique, but impersonation was the main attack vector.
Question 15 Answer: C - Smishing

Question 16 Answer: D - Spraying
Why D is correct: Many different usernames with the same password is the signature pattern of password spraying attacks. This technique avoids account lockouts while testing common passwords.
Question 17 Answer: B - Text message–based phishing
Why B is correct: Smishing is phishing via SMS (text messages). It's a portmanteau of "SMS" and "phishing."
Question 18 Answer: B - Purchase the most common typos for his organization's domain

Question 19 Answer: B - Brand impersonation
Why B is correct: Creating a social media account that appears to represent a legitimate company to conduct fraudulent activities (cryptocurrency scams) is brand impersonation.
Question 20 Answer: C - Disinformation
Why C is correct: Nation-state campaigns spreading false information about election integrity are disinformation - deliberately false information spread to achieve political goals.
Key Distinction: Disinformation is intentionally false (propaganda), while misinformation is accidentally false (mistakes).

Common Exam Patterns

  • Password Attacks: Focus on log patterns - spraying shows one password across many accounts, brute force shows many passwords against one account.
  • Phishing Variants: Remember the medium - vishing (voice), smishing (SMS), phishing (email).
  • Impersonation vs. Pretexting: Impersonation is claiming to be someone else, pretexting is the false scenario used.
  • Typosquatting: Look for misspelled domains with character substitutions or missing letters.
  • BEC: Business context with payment redirection or invoice fraud.

Why other options are wrong:
  • B (Phishing): While this could be part of a phishing campaign, the specific technique of using a misspelled domain is typosquatting.
  • C (Smishing): Smishing involves SMS/text messages, not email.
  • D (Watering hole): Watering hole attacks compromise legitimate sites that targets frequently visit.
Key Concept: Typosquatting specifically targets users who make typing mistakes when entering URLs. Common techniques include character substitution (0 for o), adjacent key errors, and missing letters.
Question 2 Answer: D - Vishing
Why D is correct: Vishing is phishing accomplished via voice or voicemail messages. The 'v' in vishing stands for 'voice'.
Why other options are wrong:
  • A (Whaling): Whaling targets high-level executives, not related to voice communication.
  • B (Spoofing): Spoofing is falsifying identity or source, not specific to voice phishing.
  • C (Spooning): This is not a recognized security term.
Question 3 Answer: A - A brute-force attack
Why A is correct: Multiple password attempts against a single username (admin) is characteristic of a brute-force attack, where an attacker tries various passwords until finding one that works.
Why other options are wrong:
  • B (Shoulder surfing): Involves physically observing someone entering credentials.
  • C (On-path attack): Involves intercepting communications between two parties.
  • D (Pretexting): A social engineering technique using false scenarios.
Question 4 Answer: C - Match repeated use of the same password during failed login attempts for multiple usernames
Why C is correct: Password spraying uses one or few passwords across many accounts. The key indicator is the same password being tried against multiple different usernames.
Why other options are wrong:
  • A: This describes credential stuffing, not password spraying.
  • B: This describes a brute-force attack against a single account.
  • D: This describes password variations, more typical of targeted brute-force.
Exam Pattern: Password spraying = One password, many accounts. Brute force = Many passwords, one account.
Question 5 Answer: B - Business email compromise
Why B is correct: BEC involves using apparently legitimate business emails to conduct scams. Invoice fraud with payment redirection to unauthorized accounts is a classic BEC technique.
Key Indicators of BEC: Unusual payment methods, unfamiliar contact addresses, urgency, and requests to change payment details.
Question 6 Answer: A - A watering hole attack
Why A is correct: Watering hole attacks compromise websites that targets frequently visit. Infecting ads on a site regularly visited by the target company's users is a textbook watering hole attack.
Question 7 Answer: D - The geographic location of system being logged into
Why D is correct: The geographic location of the target system doesn't help detect brute-force attacks. Brute-force detection focuses on authentication patterns, not where the target system is located.
