Section 1: Cybersecurity Objectives - The CIA Triad

Core Principle: Security professionals are responsible for protecting the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information and information systems used by their organizations.

The CIA Triad Components

Confidentiality Ensures that unauthorized individuals cannot gain access to sensitive information. This is what most people think of first when considering cybersecurity - preventing hackers from stealing sensitive data like Social Security numbers, credit cards, or military secrets.
  • Implementation Controls: Firewalls, access control lists (ACLs), encryption protocols
  • Primary Threat: Data exfiltration - when attackers gain access and remove sensitive information
  • Common Vulnerabilities: Misconfigured access controls, lost devices, insider threats
Integrity Ensures no unauthorized modifications to information or systems occur, whether intentional or unintentional. This protects against both malicious alterations and accidental corruption.
  • Implementation Controls: Hashing algorithms, integrity monitoring solutions, checksums
  • Example Threats:
    • Attackers modifying financial records for fraudulent transactions
    • Power surges causing "bit flips" that corrupt stored data
    • Users accidentally modifying critical system files
Availability Ensures information and systems are ready to meet the needs of legitimate users when requested. Systems must be operational and accessible when needed.
  • Implementation Controls: Fault tolerance, clustering, backup systems, redundancy
  • Common Threats:
    • Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks
    • Hardware failures or critical server crashes
    • Natural disasters affecting datacenters or communication circuits

Additional Security Principle: Nonrepudiation

Nonrepudiation Prevents someone from denying they performed an action, such as sending a message or authorizing a transaction. While not part of the CIA triad, it's crucial for accountability.
  • Primary Implementation: Digital signatures
  • Purpose: Allows verification that messages/actions truly originated from their purported source
  • Use Cases: Email authentication, transaction verification, audit trails
Remember the CIA triad components (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) and understand that nonrepudiation provides assurance that actions cannot be denied. These four concepts form the foundation of information security.

Five Categories of Breach Impact

1. Financial Risk

Definition: Risk of monetary damage resulting from a data breach
Direct Costs:
  • Datacenter rebuilding after physical destruction
  • Incident response and forensic analysis services
  • Legal fees and regulatory fines
  • Customer notification and credit monitoring services
Indirect Costs:
  • Lost revenue from competitors gaining proprietary information
  • Market share loss due to first-mover advantage compromise
  • Decreased stock value
Example: Lost laptop containing new product plans results in direct loss (laptop cost) plus indirect loss (competitors beating you to market)

2. Reputational Risk

Definition: Loss of goodwill among stakeholders due to negative publicity
Characteristics:
  • Difficult to quantify but long-lasting impact
  • Affects customer trust and loyalty
  • Impacts relationships with suppliers and partners
  • May influence future business decisions silently
Stakeholders Affected: Customers, employees, suppliers, investors, partners

3. Strategic Risk

Definition: Risk of becoming less effective in meeting major goals and objectives
Examples:
  • Loss of competitive advantage through stolen intellectual property
  • Inability to execute business plans due to missing data
  • Product development delays from lost research
  • Market position compromise from leaked strategic plans
Key Distinction: Threatens the organization's ongoing viability and ability to execute business strategy

4. Operational Risk

Definition: Risk to the organization's ability to carry out day-to-day functions
Impact:
  • Slowed business processes
  • Delayed customer order delivery
  • Need for manual workarounds to automated systems
  • Reduced efficiency and productivity
Distinction from Strategic Risk: Causes inefficiency and delays but doesn't threaten organizational existence

5. Compliance Risk

Definition: Risk of violating legal or regulatory requirements
Key Regulations:
  • HIPAA: Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act - protects health information
  • PCI DSS: Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard - protects credit card data
  • GDPR: General Data Protection Regulation - EU privacy law
  • SOX: Sarbanes-Oxley Act - financial reporting requirements
Consequences: Sanctions, fines, legal action, loss of business licenses
Identity Theft Considerations:
When breaches occur, they often extend beyond the organization to affect individuals. Organizations must specially protect Personally Identifiable Information (PII) including:
  • Social Security numbers
  • Bank account and credit card information
  • Driver's license numbers
  • Passport data
  • Medical records

Compensating Controls - PCI DSS Requirements

Compensating Controls Alternative controls implemented when an organization cannot meet original control requirements. Must satisfy three criteria under PCI DSS:
  1. Meet intent and rigor of the original requirement
  2. Provide similar level of defense as the original requirement
  3. Be "above and beyond" other PCI DSS requirements (cannot reuse controls)
Example: Running outdated OS on isolated network when software requires legacy system - isolation compensates for inability to patch.

Data Loss Prevention (DLP) Systems

Data Loss Prevention (DLP) Systems that enforce information handling policies to prevent data loss and theft by searching for unsecured sensitive information and monitoring network traffic for removal attempts.

DLP Deployment Types

Type Description Capabilities
Agent-based (Host-based) Software agents installed on individual systems ‚Ä¢ Search for stored sensitive data
‚Ä¢ Monitor system configuration
‚Ä¢ Block USB device access
‚Ä¢ Control user actions
‚Ä¢ Encrypt data automatically

‚Ä¢ Block unencrypted sensitive data
‚Ä¢ Apply automatic encryption
‚Ä¢ Alert on policy violations
‚Ä¢ Work with email systems

DLP Detection Mechanisms

  1. Pattern Matching:
    • Identifies telltale signs of sensitive information
    • Recognizes credit card number formats (16 digits in groups)
    • Detects Social Security number patterns (XXX-XX-XXXX)
    • Triggers on sensitive terms ("Top Secret", "Confidential")
  2. Watermarking:
    • Electronic tags applied to sensitive documents
    • Monitors for tagged content in transmissions
    • Used in Digital Rights Management (DRM) solutions
    • Enforces copyright and data ownership

Access Restrictions

Geographic Restrictions Limit access based on physical location using IP geolocation, GPS coordinates, or regional network boundaries. Prevents access from untrusted locations or countries.
Permission Restrictions Limit access based on user role or authorization level. Implements principle of least privilege - users only get access necessary for their job functions.

Key Definitions Summary

  • Gap Analysis: Review of control objectives vs. actual controls to identify gaps
  • Control Objectives: Statements of desired security state
  • Security Controls: Specific measures that fulfill security objectives
  • Data Exfiltration: Unauthorized removal of data from an organization
  • Rainbow Table Attack: Using precomputed hashes to reverse hashed values
  • Compensating Controls: Alternative controls when primary controls cannot be implemented
  • Deidentification: Removing ability to link data back to individuals
