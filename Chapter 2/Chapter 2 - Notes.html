1. Understanding Threat Actors Basic

Definition: Threat Actor

An individual or group that poses a threat to cybersecurity by attempting to exploit vulnerabilities in systems, networks, or applications to achieve their objectives.

1.1 Classification Attributes

Four Key Classification Criteria:

  • Internal vs. External: Location relative to the organization
  • Level of Sophistication/Capability: Technical skill and knowledge
  • Resources/Funding: Financial and time resources available
  • Intent/Motivation: The driving force behind the attack

1.2 Types of Threat Actors

Threat Actor Sophistication Resources Primary Motivation Key Characteristics
Unskilled Attackers
(Script Kiddies)		 Low Limited Thrill, Recognition Use pre-made tools, opportunistic attacks
Hacktivists Varies Moderate Political/Philosophical Ideologically driven, public exposure
Organized Crime High Significant Financial Gain Professional operations, profit-focused
Nation-State/APT Very High Virtually Unlimited Espionage, Political Persistent, sophisticated, zero-day exploits
Insider Threats Varies Limited Varies Already has access, knows systems
Shadow IT N/A N/A Productivity Unintentional risk, bypasses controls

Real-World Example: Anonymous Hacktivist Group

Anonymous has targeted organizations like the Church of Scientology, PayPal, and government agencies. They operate as a decentralized collective with no hierarchical structure, making them difficult to prosecute. Their Guy Fawkes mask has become an iconic symbol of hacktivism.

2. Threat Actor Motivations Intermediate

Exam Alert!

You MUST be able to match threat actors with their typical motivations. This is a high-frequency exam topic!

Common Motivations Explained

  • Data Exfiltration: Stealing sensitive information for various purposes
    • Example: Stealing customer databases, intellectual property
  • Espionage: Gathering intelligence on competitors or nations
    • Example: Nation-states stealing defense secrets
  • Service Disruption: Making systems unavailable
    • Example: DDoS attacks on banking systems
  • Blackmail/Extortion: Threatening to release data or continue attacks
    • Example: Ransomware attacks demanding payment
  • Financial Gain: Direct monetary benefit
    • Example: Credit card fraud, cryptocurrency theft
  • Philosophical/Political Beliefs: Advancing ideological causes
    • Example: Hacktivists exposing government surveillance
  • Ethical: Improving security (white-hat hacking)
    • Example: Penetration testing, responsible disclosure
  • Revenge: Personal retaliation
    • Example: Disgruntled employee deleting data
  • Disruption/Chaos: Creating disorder
    • Example: Defacing websites for notoriety
  • War: Military objectives
    • Example: Cyber warfare disrupting infrastructure

Real-World Example: Edward Snowden

Edward Snowden, a former NSA contractor, leaked classified documents in 2013. His motivations were philosophical/political (exposing surveillance programs) and ethical (believing the public had a right to know). This makes him both an insider threat and a hacktivist.

3. Threat Vectors and Attack Surfaces Intermediate

Key Definitions

Attack Surface: The sum of all possible security vulnerabilities in a system

Threat Vector: The method or pathway used by threat actors to gain access

3.1 Common Threat Vectors

Vector Type Description Common Attacks Mitigation Strategies
Message-Based Email, SMS, IM Phishing, Spam, Malware Email filtering, User training
Network-Based Wired/Wireless networks Man-in-the-middle, Eavesdropping Encryption, Network segmentation
Physical Access Direct system access Hardware tampering, Theft Physical security controls
Removable Media USB drives, external storage Malware distribution, Data theft USB blocking, Media policies
Supply Chain Vendors, MSPs, suppliers Backdoors, Compromised software Vendor assessment, Code review
Cloud Services SaaS, PaaS, IaaS Misconfigurations, API exploits Cloud security policies, CASB

3.2 Vulnerable Components

Common Vulnerabilities to Monitor:

  • Open Service Ports: Unnecessary services exposing attack surface
  • Default Credentials: Unchanged manufacturer passwords
  • Unsupported Systems: Legacy systems without patches
  • Vulnerable Software: Known CVEs in applications
  • Unsecure Networks: Unencrypted wireless, Bluetooth

Real-World Example: Stuxnet

Stuxnet was an APT attack attributed to US and Israeli governments that exploited zero-day vulnerabilities to compromise Iranian uranium enrichment facilities. It spread via USB drives (removable media vector) and targeted specific industrial control systems.

4. Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) Advanced

Definition: APT

A prolonged and targeted cyberattack in which an intruder gains access to a network and remains undetected for an extended period while stealing data.

APT Characteristics

  • Advanced: Uses sophisticated techniques and custom tools
  • Persistent: Long-term campaigns, often lasting months or years
  • Threat: Poses significant risk to targeted organizations

Exam Tip: Zero-Day Attacks

Zero-day attacks exploit vulnerabilities unknown to vendors. No patches exist because the vulnerability hasn't been discovered yet. APT actors often use zero-days they've discovered through their own research.

5. Threat Intelligence Advanced

5.1 Intelligence Sources

Source Type Examples Advantages Disadvantages
Open Source (OSINT) CISA, SANS ISC, VirusTotal Free, Community-driven May lack detail, validation needed
Proprietary/Commercial Vendor threat feeds Curated, Detailed analysis Costly, May have delays
Information Sharing Organizations ISACs, CISA AIS Industry-specific, Trusted Membership requirements
Dark Web Underground forums, Markets Early warning of breaches Difficult to access, verify

5.2 Threat Intelligence Assessment

Three Key Assessment Criteria:

  1. Timeliness: Is the information current and relevant?
  2. Accuracy: Can we trust the source and verify the data?
  3. Relevance: Does it apply to our environment and systems?

5.3 Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)

Common IoC Types:

  • File hashes (MD5, SHA-256)
  • IP addresses and domains
  • URLs and file paths
  • Registry keys
  • Network traffic patterns
  • Log signatures

5.4 STIX and TAXII

  • STIX (Structured Threat Information eXpression): Standardized language for describing cyber threat information
  • TAXII (Trusted Automated eXchange of Intelligence Information): Protocol for transmitting STIX data

6. Supply Chain Security Advanced

Critical Exam Topic!

Supply chain attacks are increasingly common. Know the three main types: Hardware providers, Software providers, and Service providers (MSPs).

Supply Chain Threat Vectors

  • Hardware Providers:
    • Backdoors inserted during manufacturing
    • Tampering during shipping
    • Counterfeit components
  • Software Providers:
    • Malicious code in updates
    • Compromised development environments
    • Vulnerable third-party libraries
  • Service Providers (MSPs):
    • Leveraging MSP access to customer networks
    • Compromised management tools
    • Credential theft

Real-World Example: SolarWinds Attack

The 2020 SolarWinds supply chain attack involved malicious code inserted into legitimate software updates. This affected thousands of organizations including government agencies and Fortune 500 companies. The attackers (likely nation-state) gained access to SolarWinds' build system and inserted backdoors into signed software updates.

  • Advanced techniques
  • State-sponsored (usually)
  • Persistent over time

  • Timely
  • Accurate
  • Relevant

8. Critical Terms Summary

Must-Know Definitions

  • Script Kiddie: Unskilled attacker using pre-made tools
  • APT: Advanced Persistent Threat - sophisticated, long-term attacks
  • Zero-Day: Vulnerability unknown to vendor, no patch available
  • Shadow IT: Unauthorized technology used by employees
  • IoC: Indicators of Compromise - evidence of an attack
  • OSINT: Open Source Intelligence - publicly available threat data
  • ISAC: Information Sharing and Analysis Center
  • STIX: Structured Threat Information eXpression
  • TAXII: Trusted Automated eXchange of Intelligence Information
  • MSP: Managed Service Provider
